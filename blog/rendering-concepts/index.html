Rendering Concepts

https://github.com/reactwg/react-18/discussions/46

Discrete Events

Events that occur sequentially where each event contributes to the final outcome. For example, in a video game, a player’s final score is determined by all the points earned and penalties received throughout the game. Similarly, when making coffee, the final taste depends on multiple steps - the quality of beans, grinding consistency, water temperature, and brewing time all affect the end result.

Continuous Events:

Events that happen over time where only the final state matters. Think of painting a wall - even though you may apply multiple coats of paint throughout the day, only the final color is visible and relevant. Similarly, when saving a document, each save overwrites the previous version, making the latest save the only one that matters.

Another example is adjusting your car seat position - you might make several small adjustments, but only the final position affects your driving experience.

Promise Tick

Promise is like when you go to food mall and order something to each. The attendant will give you a token number and call your name when your food is ready and attendant gets a chance to call your name. You can think of token number as promise and the act of calling the token number when attendant has time and your food is ready can be thought as Tick.

Synchronous, debouncing, throttling and cooperative

Synchronous means immediate reaction to an event. If your friend is telling you a story and you are immediately responding to his each sentence then it is called synchronous. This is not good as your friend will take too much time to complete the story.

Debouncing

Debouncing mean you will wait for particular time after your friend has paused. This strategy is good if your friend is occasionally making pauses, but your friend is not pausing, it means it will not let you respond at all.

Throttled:

Instead of waiting for your friend to pause, you will wait for particular amount of time and then respond right after sentence of your friend is complete. This is similar to synchronous where you are responding just after the friends sentence complete everytime, here you are adding delay.

There is still problem in this strategy what if your friend stops in between before 1 min, but you are not responding because your 1 min is not over.

Cooperative events:

It means you agree with your friend that he can interrupt you whenever he wants and start talking, in that case you abandon your response.

Your React components transform information into UI. There are two types of information.

Some of this information never changes during a user session. For example, your website layout, the icons on your buttons, and the order of sections on the page.

Other information may change in response to the user interaction. For example, is a button hovered or not. How many times the user clicked “add”. The content of their shopping cart or a message they were typing.

Information that changes in response to user interactions is called “state”.

Any information has some lifetime. It’s attached to something. For example, regular JS variables are attached to the function calls. They only live for so long as you’re inside this function. When you exit a function, variables from that call stop “existing.” (Aside from closures, which occur when you nest functions, and which let a nested function “keep seeing” variables from the outer function call.)

In React, each time a component renders, it’s a separate function call. So if you tried to store some information like shopping cart content in a regular variable, it would simply disappear on next render. This is why React has a concept of “state variables” you define with useState. They are associated with a place in the tree — like, a particular text input or a shopping cart you see on the screen — rather than with a function call.

Where you declare a state variable matters. Here’s how to think about it. If you render something in two places on the screen, would you want them to be synchronised or not?

For example, rendering two comment fields doesn’t mean that you want one to update when you type in the other one. So it makes sense to place state inside the comment field. Each copy is independent.

But maybe you’re rendering an already submitted comment in two places. If you make an edit to one of them, and save it, it would be reasonable to expect that both of them need to reflect your edit. So the information about a list of comments needs to live somewhere outside your component — maybe, in their shared parent or in some kind of a cache.

This is what people mean by different kinds of state. These aren’t technical terms. But “UI state” usually means some state specific to a concrete UI widget. Like text input’s text. Whereas “app state” usually means some information that’s shared between many components and needs to be in sync. So it’s usually placed outside of them.

What is reactive programming in term of frontend development?

Reactive programming is a way to implement UI where UI updates itself when underlying state changes, rather than updating the UI manually or pragmatically.
